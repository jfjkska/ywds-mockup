Assessment, intervention and school support

Learning Needs Assessment £300 · ages 6–15

Standardised, psychometric assessments help identify barriers to learning in school and enable intervention to be targeted effectively to your child's needs and abilities.

An LNA takes approximately two hours and enables me to hypothesise why your child might have difficulty with progress, attainment, achievement or attendance. You receive a report detailing your child's strengths and areas for development, an analysis of barriers to learning, and recommendations and strategies to make learning more accessible. It does not offer a diagnosis.

1-1 Literacy Intervention £50/hour · ages 7–16

Standard, evidence-based intervention for children experiencing literacy difficulties: 1-to-1, hour-long sessions that are cumulative, individually tailored, structured and multi-sensory, using the DALP framework with children aged 7–16.

First session free

The first session uses the DALP's Diagnostic Placement Screening to identify gaps in knowledge and create a targeted plan — at no charge.

What the DALP covers

The DALP framework focuses on phonemic awareness, coding subskills (syllable types and grapheme–phoneme correspondence), punctuation and syntax, suffixing, coding attack patterns, and metacognition — helping a learner understand how they think and learn.

Literacy

What is literacy — and why does it matter?

What is literacy?

Literacy is the ability to read, write, speak and listen in a way that allows us to communicate effectively and make sense of the world around us.

The importance of literacy

Literacy is a fundamental life skill. Without it, life would be very challenging. Literacy skills help you to develop and communicate — and they underpin most learning at school. Reading, writing, listening and speaking are integral to success and to day-to-day engagement with learning activities.

"The more that you read, the more things you will know. The more that you learn, the more places you'll go."
— Dr. Seuss

Dyslexia

Understanding dyslexia

What it is, how to spot it, and how to get support.

What is dyslexia?

Dyslexia is a specific learning difficulty that primarily affects reading and writing skills — but it doesn't only affect those. Dyslexia is really about information processing: dyslexic people may have difficulty processing and remembering information they see and hear, which can affect learning and the acquisition of literacy skills. It can also impact other areas, such as organisational skills. Approximately 10% of children have dyslexia.

The Delphi definition (2024)

"Dyslexia is a set of processing difficulties that affect the acquisition of reading and spelling." — the headline definition from the 2024 Delphi consensus study (Carroll et al.), which brought a wide range of experts together to reduce confusion about how dyslexia is defined.

Signs of dyslexia

Primary ages 5–11

  • Learning letters
  • Hearing the sounds in words, rhyme, etc.
  • Pronouncing words, especially longer words
  • Spelling and copying
  • Slow reading, poor decoding and/or comprehension
  • Sequencing stories; learning times tables
  • Left/right confusion
  • Organisation difficulties
  • Remembering instructions; not finishing work

Secondary ages 11–16

  • Poor reading fluency and comprehension
  • Reluctant reader
  • Remembering instructions, phone numbers, etc.
  • Slower handwriting speed
  • Poor organisation and expression of written work
  • Remembering and meeting deadlines
  • Low self-esteem; daydreaming
  • Discrepancy between oral and written ability

Ways to get support for children

  • Understand the signs of dyslexia and the difficulties associated with it
  • Intervention using a targeted, structured programme that meets individual needs
  • Appropriate assistive technology
  • Give time to process information and formulate an answer
  • Alternative methods of recording ideas and work
  • Talk to your school's SENDCo

SpLDs & co-occurrence

Specific Learning Difficulties

SpLDs rarely travel alone — understanding co-occurrence gives the full picture.

What are Specific Learning Difficulties?

A Specific Learning Difficulty (SpLD) is a neurodevelopmental condition affecting an individual's ability to receive, process and recall information — a difference or difficulty with particular aspects of learning. The term covers a range of learning difficulties:

  • Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD) / dyspraxia
  • Dyscalculia
  • ADHD (considered both a mental health difficulty and a SpLD)

An individual may have one SpLD or show signs of more than one. SpLDs are not connected to intellectual ability, or to social, economic or cultural background. Having a SpLD doesn't mean an individual can't achieve academically — but it will be harder and require greater effort. Understanding strengths and areas for development helps individuals build a "toolkit" of strategies suited to their learning preferences and needs.

Neurodiversity & SpLDs

Neurodiversity recognises normal variations in brain function that occur naturally across the human population — every one of us has a unique brain. It encompasses all SpLDs, many of which co-occur or overlap. Under the neurodiversity "umbrella", the following may also co-occur alongside a SpLD:

  • Autism
  • Developmental Language Disorder (DLD)
  • Obsessive Compulsive Disorder
  • Tourette's Syndrome
  • Anxiety
  • Speech, language & communication disorders

Dyslexia & speech, language and communication needs

Many children with dyslexia do not have speech, language and communication needs (SLCN). A child with SLCN has a broader difficulty with language — their receptive skills (understanding language) or expressive skills (use of spoken language) are impaired.

However, there is a higher rate of SLCN among children who have dyslexia. Depending on age and a range of other factors, experts estimate that between 14% and 50% of children with dyslexia also show signs of additional speech and language difficulties (McArthur et al., 2000; Catts et al., 2005).

Dyslexia & mental health

Dyslexia is not neurologically linked to mental health difficulties. However, having dyslexia can cause stress, anxiety and low mood, because it can make learning difficult and inaccessible — causing frustration and sometimes exhaustion. Children with dyslexia are at higher risk of developing mental health difficulties (NIHR, 2020). Reading difficulties can lead to mental health difficulties through mechanisms such as low self-esteem, stigma and bullying — particularly for depression and anxiety. Equally, mental health difficulties can worsen existing reading difficulties through aversion to reading and an expectation of failure (NIHR, 2020).

Lost in the system: Councils' blind spot on dyslexia

A new report from the British Dyslexia Association makes for sobering reading. Lost in the system: Councils' blind spot on dyslexia found that although around 900,000 schoolchildren in England have dyslexia — roughly three in every classroom — fewer than 2% of local authorities could say how many dyslexic children live in their area. Only three councils could provide any figures at all.

The gaps don't stop at counting. Fewer than half of local authorities employ specialists to assess and support dyslexic learners, and around a third provide no guidance at all on identifying or supporting dyslexia. Support depends far too much on where you happen to live.

The impact shows up clearly in attainment. In 2024, 26% of 11-year-olds left primary school without meeting the expected reading standard. For pupils with specific learning difficulties, only 21% met expected standards at Key Stage 2, and just one in five achieved a Grade 5 or above in GCSE English and maths.

As the BDA's chief executive Ellen Broomé put it: "Dyslexia cannot remain invisible in our schools." The BDA is calling on government and local authorities to record dyslexia consistently in education data, so support can be planned and targeted properly.

What this means for families

If the system isn't set up to spot dyslexic children, it falls to the adults around them — parents, carers and teachers — to notice the signs early and push for support. If something doesn't feel right about your child's reading, spelling or confidence, you don't have to wait: talk to your school's SENDCo, and consider an assessment to identify exactly where the barriers are.
